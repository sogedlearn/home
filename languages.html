Where They Live

Guna Yala:
The Archipelago Comarca

Guna Yala is a semi-autonomous indigenous comarca on Panama's northeastern Caribbean coast. It includes a narrow strip of mainland and an archipelago of more than 365 islands, only 49 of which are inhabited.

The comarca borders the Caribbean Sea to the north, the San Blas mountain range and Colombia to the south, Colón to the west, and Darién to the east. Its administrative capital is El Porvenir.

Worldview

The Universe According to the Guna People

For the Guna, the universe — o'Gunagui — is a living, sacred being. Nabgwana, or Mother Earth, sustains all beings in a web of spiritual and material interdependence.

Nabgwana (Mother Earth)

A fundamental living being. The Guna care for the land as a sacred commitment to past and future generations.

Demar — The Sea

The ocean is a source of life, food, and spirituality. The islands are extensions of Demar's body, inhabited with its permission.

Balance Between Being and Environment

Individual well-being depends on collective balance. There is no separation between human health and the health of the ecosystem.

Ancestral Textile Art

The Mola:
Identity Woven in Layers

Layer Technique (Reverse Appliqué)

Molas are created by stacking up to seven layers of fabric in different colors, then cutting and hand-stitching to reveal the layers beneath with millimeter precision.

Symbolic Meaning

Patterns represent elements of nature (birds, fish, plants), spiritual figures, or scenes from daily life. Each mola is unique and tells a story.

Women's Tradition

Guna women create the molas, which are part of traditional dress. The level of detail reflects the skill and social standing of the artist.

Heritage of Humanity

UNESCO recognized Mola art as Intangible Cultural Heritage, highlighting its exceptional value for all of humanity.

Autonomy & Governance

The Guna General Congress:
Ancestral Democracy

The Guna have a unique form of self-governance in Latin America. The Guna General Congress is the highest authority of the people — an institution that combines community assembly with the spiritual leadership of the Sahila (chief).

The Guna Revolution of 1925

On February 25, 1925, the Guna people rose against oppression by the Panamanian state, which sought to ban their dress, language, and customs. Led by the hero Nele Kantule, the revolution resulted in the Narganá Bay Treaty, which recognized Guna autonomy.

Autonomous Comarca Since 1953

In 1953, Panama's Law 2 officially recognized the San Blas Comarca (today Guna Yala), granting formal self-governance, territorial control, and the right to maintain traditional laws within their territory.

The Sahila: Leadership Through Song

The Sahila does not only lead — they also sing. Congress sessions include ritual chants through which the leader transmits ancestral law. The people respond collectively, creating governance that integrates the spiritual with the political.

The Dulegaya Language

Dulegaya (the people's language) is spoken by nearly 80,000 people. It is a living language passed down at home and in bilingual intercultural schools throughout the comarca.

Fishing & Agriculture

The traditional economy is based on fishing in the archipelago and growing plantain, yuca, and coconut on the mainland. The relationship with the sea and rainforest is both daily and ceremonial.

Tourism & Sovereignty

The Guna control who enters their comarca and how tourism is conducted. Visitors pay communities directly, and rules of conduct are set by the Guna Congress.

Traditional Women's Dress

Guna women wear the mola on their blouse, along with a headscarf, bead rings on wrists and ankles, and gold nose rings. It is everyday dress, not only ceremonial.
